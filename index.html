“Bridging histology, omics imaging, and AI to enable next-generation integrative pathology.”

I am a Professor at Sorbonne University specializing in biomedical image analysis, pattern recognition, and data analytics, with a strong focus on machine learning and deep learning. I also serve as a Principal Investigator at the Paris Brain Institute, within the Inria team Aramis Lab.

My research centers on microscopic biomedical image analysis - in particular computational integrative pathology, as well as pattern recognition, machine and deep learning, with a specific interest in innovative, frugal and explainable artificial intelligence methods.

Thank you for visiting my page. I look forward to sharing my research and work with you.

Purpose of our studies

Anatomopathology remains the cornerstone of diagnosis and prognosis in many diseases, serving as a critical source of ground truth in clinical decision-making. While rooted in a long-standing medical tradition, histopathology is currently undergoing a profound transformation driven by digital pathology, whole-slide imaging, and large-scale computational analysis.

Positioned at the intersection of clinical data, medical imaging, and multi-omics, anatomopathology has become a strategic domain for next-generation precision medicine. In particular, the emergence of omics imaging—including spatial transcriptomics and spatial proteomics—enables the mapping of molecular information directly onto tissue architecture, opening new avenues for understanding disease heterogeneity and microenvironmental interactions.

In this context, our research focuses on state-of-the-art artificial intelligence approaches, including deep learning, foundation models, and multimodal representation learning, to efficiently analyze and interpret whole-slide images. We develop integrative frameworks that link histomorphological patterns with molecular profiles, enabling predictive and mechanistic insights into disease progression.

A key direction of our work is the development of virtual staining and computational imaging techniques, allowing the in silico generation of molecular or immunohistochemical contrasts directly from standard H&E slides. These approaches aim to reduce costs, accelerate workflows, and extend access to advanced diagnostics, while maintaining clinical relevance and robustness.

We also actively investigate the integration of spatial transcriptomics with histopathology through generative and physics-informed models, enabling the prediction and reconstruction of gene expression landscapes from tissue morphology. This multimodal fusion represents a major step toward truly integrative pathology.

A central aspect of our work is the design of explainable, trustworthy, and human-centered AI systems. We develop interactive frameworks that involve clinicians and biomedical experts in the analytical loop, providing interpretable outputs, uncertainty quantification, and decision support aligned with emerging regulatory and ethical standards.

Ultimately, our goal is to contribute to a more dynamic, transparent, and responsible computational pathology paradigm, enhancing clinical workflows and supporting the transition toward precision and personalized medicine.

These principles of responsible and explainable AI are inherently transferable across biomedical domains. Please feel free to get in touch to explore potential collaborations.
